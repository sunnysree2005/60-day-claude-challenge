Headline Detective

Headlines are written to make you click — sometimes at the cost of accuracy. Writers may exaggerate numbers, imply causation where there's only correlation, or use dramatic words that the article itself doesn't actually support.

This matters because most people only ever read the headline. If it misleads you, that misinformation sticks — even if the article later hedges or clarifies.

Everyday application: Before sharing or reacting to a headline, ask "does the article actually prove this, or does the headline just imply it?"

Emotion Detector

Content that makes you feel something — outrage, fear, awe, envy — spreads faster and gets remembered longer. Creators and publishers know this, so they often choose words specifically to trigger a strong emotional reaction rather than to inform.

This matters because when we're emotionally activated, we think less critically. We're more likely to share first and fact-check never.

Everyday application: When a post makes you feel a strong emotion instantly, pause. Ask "what word or image did that? Would I still feel this way if it were phrased neutrally?"

Three practical habits to build

  • 1
    Pause before sharing anything that makes you feel a strong, immediate emotion — outrage and awe are both designed to bypass your judgment.
  • 2
    Read past the headline. If the article's actual evidence is softer than the headline's claim, treat the headline as misleading.
  • 3
    Ask "who benefits from me believing this?" — attention, ad revenue, and outrage clicks all reward exaggeration.
